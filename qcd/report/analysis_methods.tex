\section*{1. Autocorrelation Function}

For a sequence of measurements $\{O_0, O_1, O_2, \dots, O_{N-1}\}$, the autocorrelation function is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\Gamma(t) = \frac{1}{N-t} \sum_{i=0}^{N-t-1} (O_i - \langle O \rangle)(O_{i+t} - \langle O \rangle)
\end{equation}

where the average is

\begin{equation}
\langle O \rangle = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} O_i
\end{equation}

\section*{2. Normalized Autocorrelation Function}

The normalized autocorrelation function is:

\begin{equation}
\rho(t) = \frac{\Gamma(t)}{\Gamma(0)}
\end{equation}

where $\Gamma(0)$ is the variance of the data:

\begin{equation}
\Gamma(0) = \text{Var}(O) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} (O_i - \langle O \rangle)^2
\end{equation}

\section*{3. Integrated Autocorrelation Time}

The integrated autocorrelation time characterizes the number of effectively independent measurements:

\begin{equation}
\tau_{\text{int}} = \frac{1}{2} + \sum_{t=1}^{W} \rho(t)
\end{equation}

where $W$ is the cutoff window. In our analysis, we use $W = N/2$.

\section*{4. Statistical Error}

The error on the mean, accounting for autocorrelation, is:

\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\langle O \rangle} = \sqrt{\frac{2\tau_{\text{int}}}{N}} \sigma_O
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_O = \sqrt{\Gamma(0)}$ is the standard deviation.

\section*{5. Plaquette Observable}

The plaquette is the fundamental Wilson loop around a single plaquette:

\begin{equation}
P_{\mu\nu}(x) = \text{Tr}\left[ U_{\mu}(x) U_{\nu}(x+\hat{\mu}) U_{\mu}^{\dagger}(x+\hat{\nu}) U_{\nu}^{\dagger}(x) \right]
\end{equation}

The average plaquette:

\begin{equation}
\langle P \rangle = \frac{1}{N_t N_s^3 \cdot 6\cdot 3} \sum_{x} \sum_{\mu<\nu} \text{Re}\left[ P_{\mu\nu}(x) \right]
\end{equation}

\section*{6. Topological Charge}

The topological charge is computed using the clover field strength tensor:

\begin{equation}
Q = \frac{1}{32\pi^2} \sum_x \sum_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} 
\varepsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} \text{Tr}\left[ F_{\mu\nu}(x) F_{\rho\sigma}(x) \right]
\end{equation}

The field strength tensor is constructed from 4 oriented plaquettes at each site:

\begin{equation}
C_{\mu\nu}(x) = \frac{1}{4} \left[ P_{\mu\nu}(x) + P_{\nu,-\mu}(x) + P_{-\mu,-\nu}(x) + P_{-\nu,\mu}(x) \right]
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
F_{\mu\nu}(x) = \frac{1}{2}(C_{\mu\nu}-C_{\mu\nu}^\dagger)_{traceless} 
\end{equation}

\section*{7.  Smearing}

To improve the resolution of topological structures, we apply Stout smearing iteratively. For each smearing step, the links are updated as follows:

\begin{equation}
U_\mu(x) \to U'_\mu(x) = \frac{1}{\mathcal{N}} \left[ U_\mu(x) + \alpha \, \Sigma_\mu(x) \right]
\end{equation}

where $\Sigma_\mu(x)$ is the staple sum and $\mathcal{N}$ is a normalization factor ensuring $U'_\mu$ remains unitary after reunitarization via Gram-Schmidt.

The staple for direction $\mu$ is constructed from neighboring links:

\begin{equation}
\Sigma_\mu(x) = \sum_{\nu \neq \mu} \left[ U_\nu(x) U_\mu(x+\hat{\nu}) U_\nu^\dagger(x+\hat \mu) + 
U_\nu^\dagger(x-\hat{\nu}) U_\mu(x-\hat{\nu}) U_\nu(x-\hat{\nu}+\hat\mu) \right]
\end{equation}

After computing the updated link, we perform reunitarization to project back to SU(3).

\section*{12. Discussion}

The results show a striking observation: $\tau_{\text{int}}(Q) < \tau_{\text{int}}(P)$ for all parameter sets. This is opposite to the expected QCD behavior where topological charge typically has a longer autocorrelation time than the plaquette.

Possible explanations:
\begin{itemize}
\item The topological charge is frozen in the trivial sector ($Q \approx 0$)
\item The lattice volumes are too small to observe topological transitions
\item The Wilson flow scale $w_0$ may not be properly set
\end{itemize}

At $\beta=6.3$, the plaquette autocorrelation time decreases significantly, indicating the system is in the deconfined phase.

Near the phase transition, the integrated autocorrelation time diverges:

\begin{equation}
\tau_{\text{int}} \propto \xi^z \sim |\beta - \beta_c|^{-z}
\end{equation}

where $\xi$ is the correlation length and $z \approx 2$ for local algorithms.

\section*{13. Fractional Topological Charge in $SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N$ Gauge Theory}

Pure $SU(N)$ gauge theory possesses a $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form (center) symmetry.
Gauging this symmetry introduces a $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-form gauge field $B_{\mu\nu}$ on the lattice,
which renders the topological charge fractional:
\begin{equation}
  Q \;=\; \underbrace{-\,\frac{\varepsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\,B_{\mu\nu}B_{\rho\sigma}}{8N}}_{\text{bundle label (fixed by }B_{\mu\nu}\text{)}}
       \;+\; \underbrace{n}_{\text{instanton number (from }U\text{)}}
  \,,\qquad Q \in \frac{\mathbb{Z}}{N}
\end{equation}
For $SU(2)/\mathbb{Z}_2$: $Q$ takes values in half-integers $\{0,\,\pm\tfrac{1}{2},\,\pm1,\,\ldots\}$.

The key motivation is \textbf{topological freezing}: in standard HMC the instanton number $n$ changes via tunneling over a barrier $\propto 1/a^2$ that diverges toward the continuum limit, while the bundle label $B_{\mu\nu}$ can be updated freely as a discrete variable.

\subsection*{13.1 $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-Form Symmetry}

\subsubsection*{Definition}

A \textbf{1-form symmetry} acts on line operators (Wilson loops) rather than point operators.
Its conserved current is a 2-form $J_{\mu\nu}$, and the symmetry operator $U_\Sigma(k)$
is supported on a codimension-2 closed surface $\Sigma$.
It acts on a Wilson loop $W_R(C)$ when the linking number $\mathrm{lk}(\Sigma,C)=1$:
\begin{equation}
  U_\Sigma(k)\,W_R(C) = e^{2\pi i k\, n_R/N}\,W_R(C)
\end{equation}
where $n_R$ is the N-ality of representation $R$.

\subsubsection*{Center of $SU(N)$}

$Z(SU(N)) = \{\omega^k I_N : k=0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$ with $\omega = e^{2\pi i/N}$,
so $Z(SU(N))\cong\mathbb{Z}_N$.
The proof uses the irreducibility of the fundamental representation and Schur's lemma:
any matrix commuting with all of $SU(N)$ must be $\lambda I_N$ with $\lambda^N=1$.

\subsubsection*{Three Distinct Symmetries: from Global to Gauged}

The $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form symmetry follows the same logic as ordinary gauge theory:
it begins as a \textbf{global} symmetry of the action, is then \textbf{promoted to local},
and a new gauge field $B_p$ is introduced to restore invariance.
To see why this works, we must first distinguish three transformations
that act at different levels and leave the action invariant for \emph{different reasons}.

\bigskip
\textbf{(A) Standard $SU(N)$ gauge symmetry --- acts on sites, conjugates links.}

Parameter: $g(x) \in SU(N)$, one per site. Transformation:
\begin{equation}
  U_\mu(x) \;\to\; g(x)\,U_\mu(x)\,g(x{+}\hat\mu)^{-1}
\end{equation}
This is a conjugation from both endpoints. The plaquette transforms as:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mu\nu}(x) \;\to\; g(x)\,P_{\mu\nu}(x)\,g(x)^{-1}
\end{equation}
and the trace $\mathrm{tr}\,P$ is invariant because the trace is cyclic.
This is the familiar lattice gauge invariance --- it works for \emph{any} $g(x)$,
not just center elements. The $\mathbb{Z}_N$ center is just a subgroup; plugging
$g(x) = z\,\mathbf{1}_N$ gives $z\,U_\mu\,z^{-1} = U_\mu$ --- trivially nothing happens.
This tells us nothing new.

\bigskip
\textbf{(B) $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form symmetry (global) --- acts on links, left-multiplies.}

This is a \textbf{completely different} transformation, \emph{not} a special case of (A).
It multiplies all links crossing a fixed codimension-1 hyperplane by a center element:
\begin{equation}
  U_\mu(x) \;\to\; e^{2\pi ik/N}\,U_\mu(x) \qquad
  \text{(for all $x$ with $x_\mu = x_0$, fixed $\mu$)}
\end{equation}
This is a \emph{left-multiplication}, not a conjugation.
It is not generated by any site-based gauge transformation --- there is no $g(x)$ that
produces a left-multiply without a compensating right-multiply from the next site.

\emph{Why is the action still invariant?}
A plaquette in the $(\mu,\nu)$ plane at the hyperplane $x_\mu = x_0$ contains
two $\hat\mu$-links: one going forward (picks up $e^{+2\pi ik/N}$) and one going backward
(picks up $e^{-2\pi ik/N}$). These cancel. This is a \textbf{geometric} argument ---
the plaquette is a closed contractible loop, so it crosses the hyperplane
an equal number of times in each direction. The cancellation has nothing to do with
conjugation or trace cyclicity.

\textbf{Key distinction}:
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
\hline
 & (A) $SU(N)$ gauge & (B) $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form global \\
\hline
Parameter lives on & sites & links (hyperplane) \\
Acts on $U_\mu$ by & conjugation $g\,U\,g^{-1}$ & left-multiply $z\,U$ \\
Why $\mathrm{tr}\,P$ invariant & trace cyclicity & forward/backward cancellation \\
Applies to & any $SU(N)$ element & center elements $z \in \mathbb{Z}_N$ only \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\bigskip
\textbf{(C) $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form symmetry (gauged) --- local, requires $B_p$.}

Promoting (B) to a \emph{local} symmetry means allowing an independent
$\lambda_\mu(x) \in \{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$ on \emph{every} link:
\begin{equation}
  U_\mu(x) \;\to\; e^{2\pi i\lambda_\mu(x)/N}\,U_\mu(x)
\end{equation}
Now the four links of a plaquette carry four independent phases, and their sum
$(d\lambda)_p = \lambda_\mu(x) + \lambda_\nu(x{+}\hat\mu) - \lambda_\mu(x{+}\hat\nu) - \lambda_\nu(x)$
need not vanish. The plaquette picks up a net phase $e^{2\pi i(d\lambda)_p/N}$ and the
standard action $\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{tr}\,P$ is \textbf{no longer invariant}.
This is resolved by introducing $B_p$ (described in the gauging section below).

\subsubsection*{Why $F_{\mu\nu}$ has N-ality 0 and the Link has N-ality 1}

Under the 1-form transformation (B), the link picks up one uncompensated phase ---
it carries N-ality $n_R = 1$.
More precisely, $U_\mu(x)$ is a matrix in the \textbf{fundamental representation}:
it parallel-transports a vector $v \in \mathbb{C}^N$ from site $x{+}\hat\mu$ to $x$,
so it carries one fundamental index.

The plaquette $P_{\mu\nu}$, being a closed loop, has all phases cancel ---
effective N-ality 0.
In the continuum limit $P_{\mu\nu} \approx e^{ia^2 F_{\mu\nu}}$,
so $F_{\mu\nu}$ inherits N-ality 0. This can also be seen algebraically:
$F_{\mu\nu} = F_{\mu\nu}^a T^a$ where $T^a$ are acted on by conjugation
$T^a \to z\,T^a\,z^{-1} = T^a$ (the adjoint action), which is trivial for center elements.

\textbf{Summary}:
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
\hline
Object & Under 1-form (B) & Phase & N-ality \\
\hline
Link $U_\mu$ & $U_\mu \to z\,U_\mu$ (left-multiply) & $e^{2\pi ik/N}$ & 1 \\
Plaquette $P_{\mu\nu}$ (closed loop) & forward/backward cancel & 1 & 0 \\
$F_{\mu\nu} = F^a T^a$ (continuum limit) & $T^a \to z\,T^a\,z^{-1}$ & 1 & 0 \\
Polyakov loop (non-contractible) & crosses hyperplane once, net & $e^{2\pi ik/N}$ & 1 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsubsection*{N-ality}

The N-ality of an irreducible representation $R$ with Young diagram $(\lambda_1\ge\lambda_2\ge\cdots)$ is
$n_R = \sum_i\lambda_i \pmod{N}$.
Under a center element: $\rho_R(\omega^k I_N) = e^{2\pi ikn_R/N}\cdot\mathbf{1}_{d_R}$.

In the fractional-$Q$ papers, N-ality matters because the lattice action uses the
\textbf{fundamental plaquette} ($n_R=1$), so the $B$-field twist factor
$e^{-2\pi iB/N}$ is non-trivial. An adjoint plaquette ($n_R=0$) would decouple from $B$ entirely.

\subsubsection*{From N-ality to Gauging: How the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-Form Field Enters}

As shown in (C) above, once the 1-form symmetry is made local,
the plaquette picks up a net phase $(d\lambda)_p$ and the action breaks.
To restore invariance, introduce a $\mathbb{Z}_N$-valued 2-form gauge field
$B_p \in \{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$ on each plaquette, transforming as:
\begin{equation}
  B_p \;\to\; B_p + (d\lambda)_p \pmod{N}
\end{equation}
The \emph{gauged} action is:
\begin{equation}
  S[U,B] = \beta\sum_{x,\,\mu<\nu}\frac{1}{N}\,\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\left[
    e^{-2\pi i B_p/N}\,P_{\mu\nu}(x)
  \right]
\end{equation}
Under a $\mathbb{Z}_N$ gauge transformation, the plaquette picks up $e^{+2\pi i(d\lambda)_p/N}$
while the $B$ factor shifts to $e^{-2\pi i(B_p+(d\lambda)_p)/N}$. The two phases cancel exactly:
\begin{equation}
  e^{-2\pi i(B_p + (d\lambda)_p)/N}\cdot e^{+2\pi i(d\lambda)_p/N}\,P_p
  \;=\; e^{-2\pi iB_p/N}\,P_p
\end{equation}
So the combination $e^{-2\pi iB_p/N}P_p$ is \textbf{gauge-invariant}.

This is the discrete analog of $U(1)$ minimal coupling:
$\partial_\mu \to D_\mu = \partial_\mu - iA_\mu$ compensates for local phase rotations,
just as $B_p$ compensates for local $\mathbb{Z}_N$ rotations of the link.
The hierarchy is shifted up by one form degree:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
\hline
 & $SU(N)$ gauge (0-form) & $\mathbb{Z}_N$ gauge (1-form) \\
\hline
Parameter lives on & sites: $g(x)$ & links: $\lambda_\mu(x)$ \\
Gauge field lives on & links: $U_\mu$ & plaquettes: $B_p$ \\
Field strength on & plaquettes: $F_{\mu\nu}$ & cubes: $(dB)_c$ \\
``Matter'' that transforms & quark $\psi(x)$ & link $U_\mu(x)$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why $n_R$ is essential.}
The entire construction works because the link transforms with $n_R=1$:
it picks up a non-trivial phase $e^{2\pi i\lambda/N}$.
If we had used an adjoint plaquette ($n_R=0$), the plaquette would be invariant
under \emph{any} $\lambda_\mu(x)$ --- local or global ---
so there would be nothing to gauge and no need for $B_p$.
The $B$ field would decouple completely.

\subsubsection*{Full Form of the Gauged Action}

Combining all pieces, the lattice action coupled to the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-form gauge field is:
\begin{equation}
  S[U,B] = \beta\sum_{x,\,\mu<\nu}
  \frac{1}{N}\,\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\left[
    e^{-2\pi i B_{\mu\nu}(x)/N}\,P_{\mu\nu}(x) - \mathbf{1}
  \right]
\end{equation}
where $P_{\mu\nu}(x) = U_\mu(x)\,U_\nu(x{+}\hat\mu)\,U_\mu^\dagger(x{+}\hat\nu)\,U_\nu^\dagger(x)$
is the standard plaquette built from periodic link variables,
and $B_{\mu\nu}(x) \in \{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$ is the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-form field on each plaquette.

The phase factor $e^{-2\pi iB_{\mu\nu}(x)/N}$ is the minimal coupling that restores
local $\mathbb{Z}_N$ gauge invariance. Setting $B_{\mu\nu}(x)=0$ everywhere
recovers the standard Wilson action. The $-\,\mathbf{1}$ ensures $S=0$ for the
classical vacuum $e^{-2\pi iB/N}P = \mathbf{1}$.

Under $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form gauge transformation $\lambda_\mu(x)$:
\begin{equation}
  U_\mu(x) \to e^{2\pi i\lambda_\mu(x)/N}\,U_\mu(x)\,,\qquad
  B_{\mu\nu}(x) \to B_{\mu\nu}(x) + (d\lambda)_{\mu\nu}(x) \pmod{N}
\end{equation}
where $(d\lambda)_{\mu\nu}(x) = \lambda_\mu(x) + \lambda_\nu(x{+}\hat\mu)
- \lambda_\mu(x{+}\hat\nu) - \lambda_\nu(x)$.
The combination $e^{-2\pi iB/N}P$ is invariant, so $S[U,B]$ is gauge-invariant.

\subsection*{13.2 Continuum Origin of Fractional $Q$}

Fractional topological charge requires non-trivial spacetime topology:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
\hline
Spacetime & $H^2(M,\,\mathbb{Z}_N)$ & Fractional $Q$? \\
\hline
$\mathbb{R}^4$ & 0 (contractible) & No, $Q\in\mathbb{Z}$ \\
$S^4$ & 0 & No \\
$T^4$ & $\mathbb{Z}_N^6$ & Yes, $Q\in\mathbb{Z}/N$ \\
$\mathbb{R}^3\times S^1$ (twisted b.c.) & $\mathbb{Z}_N$ & Yes \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

On $T^4$, a gauge field need only be periodic up to a gauge transformation.
Relaxing the cocycle condition by a center element gives an $SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N$ principal bundle,
classified by 't~Hooft fluxes $B_{\mu\nu}\in\mathbb{Z}_N$ (six integers, one per plane).
Each non-trivial class forces $Q\in\mathbb{Z}/N$ by the Atiyah--Singer index theorem.

Fractional instantons are genuine BPS solutions ($F=\pm\tilde F$) with action
$S = 8\pi^2/(Ng^2)$ and charge $Q=1/N$.

\subsection*{13.3 't~Hooft Flux and Twisted Boundary Conditions}

This section traces how the 6 integers $B_{\mu\nu}$ enter the lattice action,
starting from boundary conditions on $T^4$.
The logic has four steps, and the key source of confusion is that
$B$ appears first as \textbf{6 constant integers} (from the cocycle),
then as a \textbf{position-dependent field} $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ (in the action),
and finally is \textbf{gauge-fixed back} to 6 integers at the corner.

\subsubsection*{Step 1: Transition Functions on $T^4$}

On a torus, the gauge field is periodic up to a gauge transformation.
The transition function $\Omega_\mu \in SU(N)$ (one constant matrix per direction)
specifies what happens when a link crosses the boundary in direction $\mu$:
\begin{equation}
  U_\nu(n + L\hat\mu) = \Omega_\mu\,U_\nu(n)\,\Omega_\mu^{-1}
\end{equation}
Standard periodic boundary conditions are $\Omega_\mu = \mathbf{1}$.

\subsubsection*{Step 2: The Cocycle Gives 6 Constant Integers $B_{\mu\nu}$}

At a corner of the torus where directions $\mu$ and $\nu$ meet,
one can shift a link by $L\hat\mu$ first then $L\hat\nu$, or vice versa.
Both must give the same result. Working out the two orderings:
\begin{equation}
  \Omega_\mu\,\Omega_\nu = e^{2\pi i B_{\mu\nu}/N}\,\Omega_\nu\,\Omega_\mu
\end{equation}
Since $\Omega_\mu,\Omega_\nu \in SU(N)$, the mismatch
$(\Omega_\mu\Omega_\nu)^{-1}(\Omega_\nu\Omega_\mu)$
must commute with all of $SU(N)$.
By Schur's lemma it is a center element $e^{2\pi i B_{\mu\nu}/N}\,\mathbf{1}$.

Here $B_{\mu\nu} \in \{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$ is a \textbf{single constant integer}
for each of the 6 planes $(\mu,\nu)$ on $T^4$.
It is the 't~Hooft magnetic flux --- a topological label that classifies
which $SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N$ bundle the gauge field lives in.

$B_{\mu\nu} = 0$ for all planes corresponds to $\Omega_\mu$ all commuting ---
this is the standard $SU(N)$ theory with periodic boundary conditions.

\subsubsection*{Step 3: Change of Variables --- How $g_\mu(x)$ Produces $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$}

The twisted links $\tilde U$ obey the boundary condition from Step~1:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde U(x + L\hat\mu,\,\nu) = g_\mu(x)^\dagger\,\tilde U(x,\nu)\,g_\mu(x+\hat\nu)
\end{equation}
where $g_\mu(x)$ is the position-dependent transition function in direction $\mu$.
In Step~2 we used constant $\Omega_\mu$; in general $g_\mu(x)$ can vary along the boundary face,
but its cocycle still satisfies
$g_\mu(x)\,g_\nu(x{+}L\hat\mu)\,g_\mu(x{+}L\hat\nu)^\dagger\,g_\nu(x)^\dagger
= e^{2\pi iB_{\mu\nu}(x)/N}\,\mathbf{1}$
at each boundary corner point $x$.

To do HMC, we want \textbf{strictly periodic} link variables $U(x{+}L\hat\mu,\nu) = U(x,\nu)$.
The change of variables (Eq.~(2.7) of arXiv:2501.00286) absorbs $g_\mu$ into the links:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde U(x,\mu) = \begin{cases}
    U(x,\mu)\,g_\mu(x) & x_\mu = L{-}1 \\
    U(x,\mu) & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The new $U$ are strictly periodic. But when we substitute into the Wilson action,
the boundary plaquettes that straddle $x_\mu = L{-}1$ now contain a factor of $g_\mu$
that does not cancel. This leftover is exactly the twist phase $e^{-2\pi iB_{\mu\nu}(x)/N}$:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde P(x,\mu,\nu) = e^{-2\pi iB_{\mu\nu}(x)/N}\,P(x,\mu,\nu)
\end{equation}
where $\tilde P$ is the plaquette in twisted variables and $P$ is the plaquette
in periodic variables.
\textbf{This is how $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ enters the action} ---
it is the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ phase left behind by the transition functions
after absorbing them into periodic links.

The full action in periodic variables becomes:
\begin{equation}
  S[U,B] = \beta\sum_{x,\,\mu<\nu}\frac{1}{N}\,\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{tr}\!\left[
    e^{-2\pi i B_{\mu\nu}(x)/N}\,P(x,\mu,\nu) - \mathbf{1}
  \right]
\end{equation}

The relation between $g_\mu(x)$ and $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $g_\mu(x)$ is an $SU(N)$-valued function on the boundary ---
        it contains much more information than $B$
  \item $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ part of the cocycle of $g_\mu$ ---
        it is the center phase that survives after the $SU(N)$ parts cancel in the action
  \item For constant $g_\mu = \Omega_\mu$: the cocycle gives a single constant $B_{\mu\nu}$,
        and only the boundary plaquettes are twisted
  \item For position-dependent $g_\mu(x)$: the cocycle gives $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$
        that can vary along the boundary
\end{itemize}

Now $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ is a position-dependent integer field.
In principle it can be distributed across many plaquettes, as long as the
total flux per plane equals $B_{\mu\nu}$:
total flux per plane equals $B_{\mu\nu}$:
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{x}\,B_{\mu\nu}(x) = B_{\mu\nu} \pmod{N}
\end{equation}
The distribution of $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ across plaquettes is $\mathbb{Z}_N$ gauge freedom ---
it is not physical.

\subsubsection*{Step 4: Gauge Fixing Back to 6 Integers (Corner Localization)}

Using the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form gauge freedom
$B_{\mu\nu}(x) \to B_{\mu\nu}(x) + \partial_\mu\lambda_\nu - \partial_\nu\lambda_\mu \pmod{N}$,
one can ``push'' all the distributed flux into a single corner plaquette per plane.
This is the gauge choice of Eq.~(2.10) of arXiv:2501.00286:
\begin{equation}
  B_{\mu\nu}(x) = \begin{cases}
    B_{\mu\nu} & x_\mu = L{-}1 \text{ and } x_\nu = L{-}1 \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Summary of the full chain}:
$$\underbrace{B_{\mu\nu}}_{\text{6 constants from cocycle}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\text{change of variables}}\;
  \underbrace{B_{\mu\nu}(x)}_{\text{distributed on plaquettes}}
  \;\xrightarrow{\text{gauge fix}}\;
  \underbrace{B_{\mu\nu}\text{ at corner}}_{\text{back to 6 integers}}$$
The starting point and endpoint are both 6 integers ---
the position-dependent $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ in the middle is just an intermediate
representation that carries gauge redundancy.

\subsubsection*{Strict vs.\ Relaxed Cocycle}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
\hline
 & Standard $SU(N)$ & 't~Hooft flux $B_{\mu\nu}\neq 0$ \\
\hline
Cocycle & $\Omega_\nu\Omega_\mu=\Omega_\mu\Omega_\nu$ & Relaxed by center element \\
Bundle & $SU(N)$ & $SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N$ \\
Gluon $A_\mu$ well-defined? & Yes & Yes \\
Quark $\psi$ well-defined? & Yes & No (picks up $e^{2\pi i/N}$) \\
$Q$ & $\in\mathbb{Z}$ & $\in\mathbb{Z}/N$ \\

\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The strict cocycle ($B=0$) is not forced by gauge theory self-consistency ---
it is forced by requiring fundamental-representation quarks to be globally well-defined.
In pure Yang--Mills, all $N^6$ bundle sectors are valid.

\subsubsection*{Why $B$ Has No Kinetic Term}

Unlike a continuous gauge field $A_\mu$ which has a kinetic term $F_{\mu\nu}^2$,
the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-form field $B_{\mu\nu}(x)$ has \textbf{no kinetic term} in the action.
The full action is just the coupling to the plaquette ---
there is no additional $(dB)^2$ term. Two reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{$B$ is discrete} --- it takes values in $\{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$.
    One cannot define a continuous derivative of an integer-valued field,
    so a conventional kinetic term is not meaningful.
    $B$ has no conjugate momentum and cannot participate in leapfrog MD.
  \item \textbf{$B$ is constrained to be flat}: the cocycle consistency
    requires the ``field strength'' of $B$ to vanish.
    Just as the ordinary lattice curl $dA$ sums a 1-form $A_\mu$ around the 4 edges
    of a plaquette, the curl $dB$ sums the 2-form $B_{\mu\nu}$ over the 6 faces of
    a 3D cube. For a cube in the $(\mu,\nu,\rho)$ directions at site $x$:
    \begin{equation}
      (dB)_{\mu\nu\rho}(x) = B_{\nu\rho}(x{+}\hat\mu) - B_{\nu\rho}(x)
        - B_{\mu\rho}(x{+}\hat\nu) + B_{\mu\rho}(x)
        + B_{\mu\nu}(x{+}\hat\rho) - B_{\mu\nu}(x)
    \end{equation}
    Flatness requires this to vanish on every cube:
    \begin{equation}
      (dB)_{\mu\nu\rho}(x) = 0 \pmod{N} \qquad \forall\; x,\,\mu,\nu,\rho
    \end{equation}

    \emph{Where does this come from?}
    The flatness of $B$ can be understood from two equivalent perspectives
    that correspond to the two origins of $B$ itself:

    \textbf{From the cocycle perspective:}
    The transition functions $\Omega_\mu \in SU(N)$ are ordinary matrices,
    and matrix multiplication is associative.
    The cocycle condition at 2D corners defines $B_{\mu\nu}$.
    At a 3D corner (where three directions $\mu,\nu,\rho$ meet),
    the three possible orderings of $\Omega_\mu\Omega_\nu\Omega_\rho$
    must all be consistent.
    Working out the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ phases, associativity forces $dB = 0 \pmod{N}$.

    \textbf{From the gauging perspective (Section~15.1):}
    $B$ was introduced by gauging the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 1-form symmetry.
    The key point is that $\mathbb{Z}_N$ is a \emph{discrete} group ---
    it has no Lie algebra, no infinitesimal generators, no continuous curvature.
    Compare with gauging a continuous symmetry like $U(1)$:
    there the gauge field $A_\mu$ can have curvature $F = dA \neq 0$,
    which describes a propagating photon.
    For a discrete group, there is no analog --- a discrete gauge field
    can only be flat.
    Physically, $dB \neq 0$ on a cube would be a $\mathbb{Z}_N$
    topological defect (vortex).
    The $SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N$ theory only changes which bundles are allowed
    (boundary conditions), not which defects exist ---
    excluding these vortices is exactly the flatness constraint.

    These two perspectives are equivalent:
    gauging the 1-form symmetry and summing over all
    't~Hooft flux sectors are the same operation,
    and both require $dB = 0$.

    The hierarchy mirrors electromagnetism:
    \begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
    \hline
     & Electromagnetism & $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-form \\
    \hline
    Gauge field & $A_\mu$ on links & $B_{\mu\nu}$ on plaquettes \\
    Field strength & $F_{\mu\nu}=dA$ on plaquettes & $(dB)_{\mu\nu\rho}$ on cubes \\
    ``Flat'' means & $F=0$ (no magnetic field) & $dB=0$ (no $B$-curvature) \\
    Consequence & $A$ locally pure gauge & $B$ locally pure gauge \\
    \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \end{table}
    So $B$ is a \textbf{flat connection} with no local propagating degrees of freedom.
    It carries only global (topological) information.
\end{enumerate}

In the path integral, $B$ is not integrated over continuously but \textbf{summed} over:
\begin{equation}
  Z = \sum_{B_{\mu\nu}\in\mathbb{Z}_N}\;\int[dU]\;e^{-S[U,B]}
\end{equation}
In HMC, this sum is sampled by randomly proposing new $B$ values
and accepting/rejecting via Metropolis --- fundamentally different from the
leapfrog evolution of~$U$.

\subsubsection*{$SU(N)$ vs.\ $SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N$}

\begin{equation}
  Z_{SU(N)/\mathbb{Z}_N} = \frac{1}{N^6}\sum_{B_{\mu\nu}\in\mathbb{Z}_N} Z_{SU(N)}(B_{\mu\nu})
\end{equation}
Local observables (including the topological susceptibility $\chi_t$) agree in the large-volume limit:
non-trivial $B$ sectors contribute corrections suppressed as $e^{-\Delta F\cdot V}$.

\subsection*{13.4 Halfway-Updating HMC for Dynamic $B$}

\subsubsection*{The Problem}

$B_{\mu\nu}\in\{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$ is discrete with no conjugate momentum ---
standard leapfrog cannot evolve it.

\subsubsection*{Algorithm}

Place the $B$ update at the midpoint of the MD trajectory:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Sample momentum $\pi\sim e^{-\pi^2/2}$
  \item \textbf{First half-MD}: evolve $\{U,\pi\}$ with
        $H=\tfrac{1}{2}\pi^2+S(U,B)$ for time $\tau/2$
  \item \textbf{Random $B$ update}: draw $B'_{\mu\nu}$ uniformly from $\{0,\ldots,N{-}1\}$
        for each of 6 planes (symmetric proposal)
  \item \textbf{Second half-MD}: evolve with $H(\cdot,B')$ for time $\tau/2$
  \item \textbf{Metropolis}: accept $\{U',B'\}$ with
        $P_A=\min[1,\,e^{-\Delta H}]$
\end{enumerate}

Detailed balance holds because forward and reverse paths are mirror images
(leapfrog reversibility + symmetric $B$ proposal).

Practical parameters: $\tau=1.0$, $\Delta\tau=0.02$, 25 leapfrog steps per half (50 total);
acceptance $\sim 86\%$ at $\beta=2.6$, $L=20$.

\subsubsection*{Why It Resolves Topological Freezing}

Since $Q_\mathrm{frac} = -\varepsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}B_{\mu\nu}B_{\rho\sigma}/(8N)$,
randomly resetting $B$ each trajectory directly shuffles the topological sector ---
no gauge-field tunneling is required.

\subsubsection*{Gradient Flow}

The flow equation uses $S[V_t,B]$ (not $S[V_t]$): it drives
$e^{-2\pi iB/N}P\to\mathbf{1}$, preserving the twist.
Flow time: $t=(0.7L)^2/8$.
After flow, ordinary plaquettes $\to\mathbf{1}$ while the corner plaquette
$\to e^{+2\pi iB/N}$ --- the fractional instanton structure is preserved.

\subsubsection*{Improved Definition}

\begin{equation}
  Q_\mathrm{imp} = c_0\,Q_\mathrm{plaq} + c_1\,Q_\mathrm{rect}\,,
  \qquad c_0=\tfrac{5}{3},\quad c_1=-\tfrac{1}{12}
\end{equation}
The rectangle-improved definition reduces $O(a^2)$ discretization errors
and converges best to half-integer values under gradient flow.

\subsubsection*{Fractional $Q$ Formula}

\begin{equation}
  Q = -\frac{1}{N}\,\frac{\varepsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\,B_{\mu\nu}B_{\rho\sigma}}{8}
      + \mathbb{Z}
\end{equation}
The fractional part is entirely fixed by $B_{\mu\nu}$.
For $SU(2)/\mathbb{Z}_2$ with $B_{12}=B_{34}=1$: $Q=\tfrac{1}{2}+\mathbb{Z}$.

In algebraic topology language: the fractional part equals
$-\frac{1}{N}\mathcal{P}_2(B)\pmod{1}$,
where $\mathcal{P}_2$ is the Pontryagin square of the $\mathbb{Z}_N$ 2-form $B$.
